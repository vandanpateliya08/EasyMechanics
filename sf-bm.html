A visual guide to understanding internal forces in beams

What Are Internal Forces?

When a beam is subjected to external loads, internal forces develop within the beam to maintain equilibrium. The two most important are Shear Force (SF) and Bending Moment (BM).

⚡

Shear Force

The algebraic sum of all vertical forces acting on one side of a cross-section.

🔄

Bending Moment

The algebraic sum of moments of all forces acting on one side of a cross-section.

📐

Sign Convention

Upward forces on the left are positive. Clockwise moments on the left are positive.

Shear Force

Shear force at any cross-section is the algebraic sum of all transverse forces acting to the left (or right) of that section.

V(x) = ΣFy (forces to the left of section)

Key Points

  • Shear force is constant between two point loads.
  • It changes abruptly at a concentrated load.
  • Under UDL, shear force varies linearly.
  • Shear force is zero at a free end with no load.

Bending Moment

Bending moment at a section is the algebraic sum of moments of all forces acting on one side about the centroidal axis.

M(x) = ΣM (moments of forces to the left of section)

Relationship Between SF and BM

dV/dx = -w(x)

Rate of change of shear force equals the negative of distributed load intensity.

dM/dx = V(x)

Rate of change of bending moment equals the shear force at that section.

d²M/dx² = -w(x)

Second derivative of bending moment equals the negative distributed load.

Key Points

  • Bending moment is maximum where shear force is zero.
  • BM is zero at free ends and simple supports.
  • Under a point load, BMD has a kink.
  • Under UDL, BM varies as a parabola.
